# LA TRAQUE DE L'ATTENTAT DU MÉTRO 14
## CHAPTER 11.5: ABDELKRIM'S RECRUITMENT

The motorcycle was a Yamaha YZF-R3. 300cc. Fast enough to feel alive. Not fast enough to escape the police.

Abdelkrim Diallo was twenty-eight years old in 2021. He had a job at a warehouse in Mantes-la-Jolie. Inventory management. Low wage. No future. He had an apartment on the fourth floor with no elevator. He had a motorcycle that made him feel free. He had Friday nights where he would ride through the streets of Mantes and pretend he was someone else. Someone important. Someone with a future. Someone with options.

On a Friday in March 2021, the police stopped him. Routine traffic control. License check. Random selection. He had no outstanding warrants. No criminal record. Nothing. Just a young man on a motorcycle who was riding twelve kilometers over the speed limit.

He panicked.

He didn't know why he panicked. Maybe because police had always intimidated him. Maybe because his father had been detained in 1995 during the Algerian immigration sweeps and had warned him: "Never trust French police. Never surrender." Maybe because something in his DNA understood that young North African men on motorcycles were always suspects.

He accelerated.

The police turned on their lights. He kept accelerating. He turned left onto Rue de Gaulle. He turned right onto Avenue de la République. He was doing one hundred twenty kilometers per hour in a sixty zone. The motorcycle felt like it was flying. The police were behind him, red lights flashing, sirens wailing.

He thought he could outrun them.

He thought he could disappear into the Mantes streets and lose them. He thought his youth and his motorcycle and his fear would combine into speed and escape.

He was wrong.

He came to a corner. He braked too hard. The back wheel skidded. The motorcycle started to slide. He compensated by leaning right. The motorcycle caught grip. But the momentum was wrong. The angle was wrong. The speed was too high.

He hit the wall at Rue Voltaire and Rue de la Mairie. The motorcycle crushed against stone. His body crushed between motorcycle and stone.

The police found him unconscious. The motorcycle was destroyed. His spine was broken at the L4-L5 junction. Complete spinal cord transection. No neurological function below the point of injury. The medical term is complete paraplegia.

In simple terms: he would never walk again.

---

The rehabilitation was six months. The depression was permanent.

The hospital in Rouen had a spinal ward. Six beds. Five other people learning to live without the use of their legs. The physical therapist would work with them. Teach them to use wheelchairs. Teach them to transfer from bed to wheelchair. Teach them to live in a horizontal world when they'd been vertical their whole lives.

Abdelkrim was angry the entire time.

He didn't cry. He didn't complain about pain (there was no pain, which was almost worse—the absence of sensation was like being already dead). He just sat in bed and watched the wall and thought about how three years ago he was on a motorcycle and three weeks from now he would be in a wheelchair for the rest of his life.

The other patients tried to bond with him. They'd exchange tips about bathroom accessibility. They'd share names of places that were wheelchair-friendly. They'd try to create a community of the disabled.

Abdelkrim rejected all of it.

He didn't want to be part of a disabled community. He wanted his life back. He wanted to be on a motorcycle. He wanted to be young and fast and alive.

None of that was possible.

By the time he was released, he hated everyone. He hated the police who chased him. He hated his father for warning him. He hated himself for panicking. He hated the doctors for keeping him alive. He hated the wheelchair he would need for the rest of his life.

By 2022, his anger had found a direction.

He started attending meetings. Political meetings. Meetings about Algerian identity and colonial history. Meetings where people talked about France's crimes in Algeria. About the torture. About the massacres. About the mothers who lost children. About the mothers who lost everything.

The meetings were in basements and community centers. There were maybe twenty people at each one. All of them angry. All of them feeling like France had wronged them or their families. All of them looking for someone else to blame.

Abdelkrim fit right in.

The rage that had been directed at himself and the police found a new target: France itself. The colonial legacy. The indifference. The refusal to apologize for what had been done to Algeria. The way France acted like it had done nothing wrong. The way France had benefited from Algerian suffering and never acknowledged it.

By early 2023, he'd become known in the network. The angry young man in the wheelchair. The one with nothing to lose. The one with rage that couldn't be satisfied by politics or speeches or academic discussions about colonial crimes.

That's when Yasmine found him.

She came to a meeting in March 2023. She didn't look like she belonged to the group. She was too young. Too focused. Too certain. She wore her anger like armor, not like a wound.

She introduced herself as Yasmine. She said she represented a more serious approach to addressing colonial injustice. She said speeches and meetings and academic discussions weren't enough. She said France needed to understand the cost of its crimes. She said France needed to bleed.

Most of the group dismissed her. They thought she was a provocateur. An undercover police agent testing them. A naive idealist looking for attention.

Abdelkrim listened.

After the meeting, she approached him.

"You're angry," she said. It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"You want things to change."

"Yes."

"You want France to pay for what it did."

"Yes."

"Then I have work for you."

She didn't explain immediately. She just gave him a number. A burner phone number. She told him to call it in three days if he was interested.

He called in two days.

---

The recruitment happened slowly.

First: philosophy. Why the bombing was justified. Why killing civilians was necessary. Why France's civilians were complicit in colonial violence. Why the deaths of some French people might finally force the government to acknowledge the deaths of thousands in Algeria.

Yasmine was brilliant at this. She quoted Frantz Fanon. She referenced the Algerian War. She connected personal trauma (Abdelkrim's paralysis, his loss of mobility, his lost future) to national trauma (France's occupation of Algeria, its extraction of resources, its extraction of lives).

She showed him how his wheelchair could become a weapon. How his disability could become useful. How his paralysis could finally mean something.

"Wheelchairs are invisible," she told him. "People don't notice them. They don't search them carefully. Nobody suspects a disabled person. Nobody expects disability to be a threat."

Abdelkrim understood. His paralysis had made him invisible. Useless. Forgotten. Suddenly, Yasmine was telling him his uselessness could be weaponized. His disability could become power.

Second: technical training. She introduced him to Hassan Medjahed. Hassan taught explosives. Hassan explained detonators. Hassan explained timers. Hassan explained how to construct a bomb that would work reliably. A bomb that would kill multiple people. A bomb that would make a statement.

Third: assignment. 

"We're placing three devices in Métro 14," Yasmine said. "Three wheelchair bombs in three different cars. The bombs will detonate simultaneously. The coordinated attack will draw attention. It will demonstrate our commitment. It will show that we are serious about forcing France to apologize for Algeria."

Abdelkrim accepted the assignment without hesitation.

He was going to be part of something. He was going to matter. For the first time since the police chase, since the crash, since the spinal cord injury, he was going to mean something.

The bomb was constructed in the Mantes laboratory. Hassan built it. Hassan showed him how to arm it. Hassan showed him the detonator, the timer, the electrical connections.

"This will work," Hassan promised. "The bomb will detonate at 6:00 AM exactly. Six thirty seconds into the morning commute. When Métro 14 between Châtelet and Madeleine is packed. When the impact will be maximum."

Abdelkrim agreed to everything.

He practiced boarding the train. He practiced moving the wheelchair into position. He practiced handling the weight of the device under his seat. He practiced acting normal. He practiced looking disabled and pathetic and invisible.

He was ready.

---

But on the night before the bombing, something happened.

Abdelkrim couldn't sleep. He sat in his apartment, in his wheelchair, looking at the device that was supposed to kill him along with maybe two hundred other people. He thought about those two hundred people. Some of them would be commuters like him. Some of them would be mothers going to work. Some of them would be students going to class. Some of them would be people who had nothing to do with France's colonial crimes. People who were just trying to get to their destinations.

He thought about his own mother. She was in Mantes. She was a cleaner. She didn't know what her son was about to do. If the bomb went wrong (if it detonated too early, if it detonated too late, if something failed), it might not kill him. It might kill him partially. It might wound him. It might leave him more damaged than he already was.

That was when the doubt started.

Abdelkrim had been angry his entire adult life since the crash. Angry at the police. Angry at France. Angry at his father. Angry at himself. But he'd never been afraid. Fear was new. Fear was his body trying to tell him something his rage wouldn't listen to.

He looked at the detonator. He looked at the electrical connection. He thought about how easy it would be to disconnect the wire. How easy it would be to make sure the bomb wouldn't work.

By 3:00 AM, he'd made his decision.

He carefully disconnected the electrical lead from the primary charge. He severed it where it wouldn't be obvious. He covered the connection point with his fingernail, abrading the metal just enough that even if someone tried to reconnect it, the connection would be weak.

He also let the battery run down. He disconnected the battery and left it disconnected overnight. By morning, the battery would be dead. Even if someone reconnected the wires, there would be no power. The detonator wouldn't fire.

It was sabotage. It was betrayal. It was the only thing Abdelkrim could do to be part of the bombing without actually participating in the killing.

He didn't tell Yasmine. He didn't tell Hassan. He just loaded the device into his wheelchair and prepared to board the train.

The last thought before he entered the Métro station was a simple one:

"Maybe this is how my anger ends. Not with death. Just with silence."
