# LA TRAQUE DE L'ATTENTAT DU MÉTRO 14
## CHAPTER 7: CHAOS

The first riot starts at 7:30 PM.

Rue Danielle Casanova. Fifth floor of an HLM. A family of six living in fifty square meters. A mother. Four children. One grandmother. Police arrive with a warrant. They're checking apartments. Looking for the cell. The mother resists. Not violently. Just refuses to open the door without a lawyer.

The police break it down anyway.

Inside, there's nothing. Just a family. Just poverty. Just people trying to live.

The children scream. The grandmother cries. The mother is arrested anyway. "Obstruction of justice," the officer writes down.

Word spreads. By 8:00 PM, there are fifty people outside the building. By 8:30 PM, there are three hundred. By 9:00 PM, the entire neighborhood is burning.

Buses are torched. Police cars are overturned. Barricades are built from burnt-out cars and furniture. The CRS responds with tear gas. Rubber bullets. Water cannons.

Thomas is watching from a command post. A mobile unit parked outside city hall. Screens showing feeds from helmet cameras. From drones. From news helicopters. The chaos is being documented. It's being broadcast live. It's becoming history in real time.

"We need to pull back," Moreau says. He's next to Thomas. Watching the screens.

"We can't," Thomas says. "The cell is still out there. We pull back, they escape."

"Look at what's happening," Moreau says. He points at a screen. CRS officers beating a teenager who threw a rock. The teenager is bleeding. The officers don't stop.

Thomas doesn't look away.

By 10:00 PM, the government makes a decision.

The announcement comes from the Minister of Interior. Live on television. Thomas watches it on a screen in the command post.

"We are declaring Mantes-la-Jolie a temporary detention zone. Any person found outside their residence after 10:00 PM will be arrested. A temporary containment facility has been established. Detainees will be processed and released once the security threat has been neutralized."

The screen switches to aerial footage. Three acres of land. Fifty shipping containers. Arranged in rows. Military precision. It's a prison made of metal boxes.

"That's..." Moreau doesn't finish.

"Expedient," Thomas says.

By 10:30 PM, the police are arresting everyone on the streets. Rioters. Residents. Journalists. A man walking home from work. A woman trying to escape the tear gas. A teenager who was just standing there.

They're all being loaded into buses. Transported to the container facility.

By midnight, there are five thousand people in the containers. By 2:00 AM, there are eight thousand.

Thomas is in the detention facility. Walking between the containers. Watching. The containers are packed. People standing shoulder to shoulder. No lights except what spills through the doors. No toilets. Just buckets. No water. Just the promise that they'll be released soon.

They won't be released soon.

A man approaches Thomas. Age maybe forty. Algerian accent.

"I live here," the man says. "I wasn't rioting. I was walking home. They arrested me anyway. Why?"

Thomas doesn't answer. He walks past.

The man follows.

"You need to let me out. I have family. I have a job tomorrow. I did nothing."

Thomas doesn't stop walking.

By 3:00 AM, Thomas realizes something. The cell could be in here. One of the eight thousand people in the containers. Yasmine. Hassan. Inès. Any of them could have been arrested in the sweep.

Or none of them.

He orders DNA testing. Blood samples from anyone matching descriptions. Facial recognition scans. The logistics are impossible. Eight thousand people. Limited resources. The facility wasn't built for processing. It was built for storage.

By dawn, the facility is a humanitarian disaster. People have pissed themselves. Some are sick. Some are violent. Some are crying. The guards are overwhelmed. They're using force. Beating people who get too close to the container doors. Beating people who ask for water.

Thomas watches this.

By 7:00 AM, Moreau finds him.

"We found something," Moreau says. "Hassan Medjahed. He was arrested during the riots. He was running from police. He matched the description. He's being held in Container Seven."

Thomas goes to Container Seven.

Hassan is sitting on the floor. Hands cuffed. Face bruised. He's not talking. He's just staring at the wall.

Thomas sits across from him.

"Where are they?" Thomas asks.

Hassan doesn't respond.

"Yasmine. Inès. Malik. Where are they?"

Hassan looks up. His eyes are empty.

"They're scattered," Hassan says. "Some still in Paris. Some heading to borders. Some already gone. Cell is split. Multiple teams. Multiple targets. They'll coordinate from different locations. You won't stop them all."

Thomas doesn't move.

"What's the next target?" Thomas asks.

"I don't know," Hassan says. The smile is that of someone who has accepted death. "But there will be more. There's always more. They're not done. France hasn't apologized yet."

Thomas stands.

He walks out of Container Seven.

Outside, the facility is chaos. More people are arriving. More buses. The containers are already full. They're doubling up. Putting two people where one should fit. The conditions are deteriorating. The guards are becoming more violent.

By 9:00 AM, Thomas is back at the command post.

He understands something: Hassan doesn't know the rest of the plan. Hassan is cell member, but not command. Yasmine keeps operational details compartmentalized. Hassan was finance and logistics. He doesn't know where they are. He doesn't know what's next.

Thomas orders immediate action.

"Full mobilization. Every border crossing. Every airport. Every port. I want them found before they can execute the next attack. They're scattered but connected. Someone knows where they're going. Someone knows the next target."

The orders are relayed. Police mobilize to borders. Airports are on alert. Ports are watched. But the cell is already scattered. Some are already gone.

Yasmine is leading them. Hassan said so. Yasmine who speaks about justice. Yasmine who sees herself as the voice of the dead. Yasmine who will keep attacking until France listens or she's dead.

Thomas looks at the map of France. The borders. The routes. The escape paths. It's a labyrinth. It's impossible. It's where the cell will try to go.

He picks up the radio.

"All units. They're running. Find them before they leave France."

Moreau is next to him.

"We don't even know which direction," Moreau says.

"South," Thomas says. "They're going south. To the Mediterranean. To Algeria. That's where they want to go."

Thomas picks up his rifle.

He's going into the darkness.
