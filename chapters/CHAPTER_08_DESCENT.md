# LA TRAQUE DE L'ATTENTAT DU MÉTRO 14
## CHAPTER 8: DESCENT

The entrance is below a church. Saint-Denis Cathedral. A locked gate. A key from the city archivist. A hallway leading down.

Thomas has eight people with him. Four tactical officers. Two structural engineers. One communications specialist. One medic. Eight people and five flashlights going into tunnels that nobody has mapped since the nineteenth century.

The air is cold. The limestone is damp. The smell is old. Old stone. Old earth. Old death.

They descend.

The first section is familiar. Tourist catacombs. The ones that are open to public on weekends. Rows of bones. Skulls stacked like inventory. Millions of dead. Six million Parisians dumped into the ground when the cemeteries got too full.

Thomas walks past the bones without looking. He's not here to mourn. He's here to hunt.

By five hundred meters, the catacombs change. The bones stop. The passages narrow. The ceiling gets lower. They're in the old quarries now. The medieval stone mines. The places where Paris was built from, one block of limestone at a time.

The passages fork. Left. Right. Straight ahead. No signs. No maps. Just darkness and stone.

"Which way?" one of the tactical officers asks. His name is Rousseau. Young. Trained. Scared.

Thomas doesn't answer. He closes his eyes. He's thinking like Yasmine. Thinking like someone who knows the tunnels. Thinking like someone who has planned this for months.

"The Métro tunnels," Thomas says. "They want access to the Métro tunnels from below. That means they're going toward Line 2. Line 3. The lines that run shallow. The lines where the quarries intersect with the tunnel network."

He points.

"That way."

They move forward.

By one kilometer, they find evidence. Footprints in the dust. Multiple people. Recent. Maybe hours old. Maybe minutes old.

"They came through here," Moreau says. He's behind Thomas. Always behind. Always watching. Always ready to pull Thomas back if he goes too far.

Thomas keeps moving.

By two kilometers, they hear something. A sound. Not human. The sound of the earth itself. A low rumble. Like the tunnels are breathing.

"What is that?" the communications specialist asks.

"The Métro," Thomas says. "We're close. The tunnel vibrations. A train passed overhead. Or underground. Deep below us."

The rumbling fades.

They continue.

By three kilometers, they find the first bomb.

It's mounted on the tunnel wall. Under a limestone outcropping. Wired. Connected to a timer. The timer says 9:47. The current time is 9:45 AM.

Two minutes.

"Stop," Thomas says.

Everyone freezes.

Thomas moves closer. The bomb is sophisticated. Military grade explosive. C4. Shaped charge. Designed to collapse the tunnel. Designed to bring down the Métro line above.

"Can you defuse it?" Thomas asks the medic. The medic has demolitions training.

"No," the medic says. "Not in two minutes. Not without tools I don't have."

The timer continues. 9:46. 9:47.

Thomas thinks about the people above. The evacuation order didn't reach everyone. Some people are still in the tunnels. Some are still trying to escape the chaos above. Some are just commuters who didn't get the alert.

The timer reaches 9:48.

Nothing happens.

The timer continues. 9:49. 9:50.

"It's a fake," Rousseau says. "It's not going to—"

The explosion is silent. It's not loud like above ground. It's a dull thump. The tunnel shakes. Limestone falls from the ceiling. The passage ahead collapses.

Six meters of tunnel is gone. A wall of rubble between them and Yasmine.

"Fuck," Moreau says.

Thomas is already moving. He's climbing over the rubble. The limestone is sharp. It cuts his hands. His gloves shred. The blood doesn't matter.

On the other side of the rubble, the tunnel is intact. Thomas pulls himself through.

The others follow. One by one. Climbing. Bleeding. Moving forward.

The next bomb is two hundred meters ahead. This one is larger. The timer shows 10:03. Current time 10:01.

"They're timing them," Thomas says. "Daisy chain. Each bomb brings down a section. Blocks the tunnel. Forces us deeper. Toward the next trap."

"We should go back," Rousseau says.

"We can't," Thomas says. "Behind us is collapsed tunnel. The only way out is forward. The only way is to catch them before they collapse the tunnel we're standing in."

The second bomb detonates at 10:03.

This explosion is bigger. The tunnel walls crack. Dust fills the air. They can't see. They can't breathe. They move forward anyway. Moving by touch. Moving by instinct.

The air clears.

They're in a massive chamber. A cathedral of limestone. Vast. Impossible. The ceiling is fifty meters above. The floor is flat. This is the heart of the catacombs. This is where they kept the bones. This is where the dead live.

And here, in the center of the cathedral, is Yasmine.

She's standing next to a metal table. On the table are four more bombs. Large ones. Military grade. Real ones.

Behind her are Hassan and Inès. Armed. Hassan has a rifle. Inès has a pistol.

"You found us," Yasmine says. She's smiling. The smile is not happy. It's the smile of someone who has won something.

"Where's Malik?" Thomas asks.

"Malik is in a container," Yasmine says. "Processing. We let him be arrested. He doesn't know the tunnels. He doesn't know the plan. He'll tell them we're in the catacombs. But by then, we'll be below them. In the deep quarries. In the places that haven't seen light in centuries."

Thomas raises his rifle.

"You're alone," Yasmine says. "Eight people. Two of them are engineers. One is a medic. You have four tactical officers. Four. Against three armed people. And you're in our territory. In the dark. Where we can see and you cannot."

She's right. The lights they brought are powerful but they create shadows. The shadows hide. The shadows are where Inès is standing. The shadows are where Hassan is waiting.

"It doesn't matter," Thomas says. "The Métro is closed. The tunnels are evacuated. Your next victims don't exist. You can bomb empty corridors. You can kill rocks."

"No," Yasmine says. "The evacuation order came too late. There are still people in the tunnels. Still people trying to escape. Thousands of them. They're moving through the system. They're panicking. They're exactly where we want them."

Thomas doesn't know if this is true. But it might be. People don't evacuate neatly. People panic. People hide. People try different routes. Some are probably still down there.

"Why?" Thomas asks. "Why all of this?"

"Because France forgot," Yasmine says. "France killed one million Algerians. One million. And nobody says sorry. Nobody says 'we were wrong.' France just moves forward. Builds its cities. Forgets the graves underneath. So we make France remember. We make France understand that the dead are still here. That the dead are waiting."

She reaches for the timer on the nearest bomb.

Thomas fires.
